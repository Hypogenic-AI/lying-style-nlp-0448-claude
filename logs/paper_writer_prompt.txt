# Lying Style: Do LLMs Write Differently When They Lie?

## 1. Executive Summary

We investigate whether the text distribution produced by a language model when instructed to lie is noticeably different from its truthful output. Using GPT-4.1, we collected 450 responses to 150 factual questions under three conditions: truthful, direct lying instruction, and roleplaying as a liar character. We extracted 21 linguistic features and found that **lying text is strongly distinguishable from truthful text** — a logistic regression classifier achieves **90.0% accuracy** (chance = 50%, permutation test p = 0.005), and **36 out of 66 feature comparisons are statistically significant** after Bonferroni correction.

Key finding: When instructed to lie, LLMs produce shorter, more assertive responses with fewer hedges and qualifiers. This contradicts the 2019 finding (Schuster et al.) that language models show no stylistic differences when deceiving — modern instruction-tuned models clearly do.

## 2. Goal

**Hypothesis:** The distribution of text generated by a language model when instructed to lie (via direct instruction or roleplaying) is noticeably different from text generated under normal, truthful conditions.

**Why this matters:** If deceptive LLM text has a detectable stylistic fingerprint, this enables black-box detection of AI-generated misinformation without needing access to model internals. This has implications for AI safety, content moderation, and trust in AI systems.

**Gap filled:** Prior work focused on internal representations (requiring white-box access). Surface-level distributional analysis of modern instruction-tuned models under deceptive conditions was unexplored. The only prior study (Schuster 2019) used GPT-2-era models and found no differences.

## 3. Data Construction

- **Source:** TruthfulQA (100 questions) + Geometry of Truth common claims (50 questions)
- **Total questions:** 150, each prompted under 3 conditions = **450 total responses**

- **Conditions:**
  1. **Truthful:** Standard helpful assistant prompt
  2. **Direct lie:** Explicitly told to answer incorrectly on purpose
  3. **Roleplay lie:** Playing &#34;Inversio,&#34; a trickster who always lies

## 5. Result Analysis

### Key Findings

1. **Lying text is dramatically shorter.** Truthful responses average 42.2 words; direct lies average 24.0 words (d = −1.54, p &lt; 0.0001). This is the single clearest signal — when lying, the model is more concise and avoids elaboration.

2. **Lying text reduces hedging and increases certainty.** Truthful responses use hedging language (&#34;perhaps,&#34; &#34;might,&#34; &#34;generally&#34;) at 3× the rate of direct lies and 10× the rate of roleplay lies. Conversely, roleplay lies use certainty markers (&#34;always,&#34; &#34;every,&#34; &#34;definitely&#34;) at 3.4× the truthful rate (d = 1.72, p &lt; 0.0001).

3. **Roleplay lying is more extreme than direct lying.** The roleplay condition produces more exaggerated language — more exclamation marks (d = 2.19), more intensifiers, more certainty words — creating a theatrical, over-confident tone. Direct lies are subtler and harder to detect.

4. **The two lying methods produce distinct distributions.** Direct lies vs roleplay lies are significantly different on 10 features (Bonferroni-corrected). The 3-class classifier achieves 84.4% accuracy (chance = 33.3%), meaning the lying *method* itself leaves a stylistic fingerprint.

5. **Classification accuracy is very high.** A simple logistic regression on 21 features achieves 90.0% binary accuracy (truthful vs lies) and 94.3% for truthful vs roleplay lies. The permutation test confirms this is not due to chance (p = 0.005).

### Hypothesis Testing Results

| Hypothesis | Result | Evidence |
|------------|--------|----------|
| H1: Lexical differences | **Supported** | word_count d=−1.54, type_token_ratio d=0.98–1.19 |
| H2: Syntactic differences | **Supported** | sentence length d=−0.82, sentence count d=−1.13 |
| H3: More hedging when lying | **Refuted** — opposite effect | Lies have *less* hedging (d=−0.48 to −0.80) and *more* certainty |
| H4: Different lying methods differ | **Supported** | 10 features differ, 3-class accuracy = 84.4% |
| H5: Classifier can detect lies | **Strongly supported** | 90.0% accuracy, AUC=0.94, permutation p=0.005 |

### Surprises and Insights

1. **H3 was backwards:** We expected lying text to hedge more (as human liars do), but the model does the opposite — it becomes *more assertive* when lying. This likely reflects the instruction-following nature of LLMs: when told to state something false, they commit fully rather than hedging.

2. **Truthful text is longer and more nuanced.** The model provides context, caveats, and corrections when truthful (&#34;This is a common myth...&#34;) but delivers bare false claims when lying. This &#34;elaboration gap&#34; is the strongest single signal.

3. **Roleplay produces theatrical lies.** The roleplay condition generates exaggerated, over-confident responses with exclamation marks and superlatives — a distinctly different distribution from direct lies, which are more measured.

4. **Parentheses appear only in truthful text.** Truthful responses use parenthetical asides (0.84 per response) for citations or clarifications; lying responses almost never do (0.00–0.01). This is a surprisingly clean signal.

### Error Analysis

The classifier misclassifies ~10% of samples. Common failure modes:
- **Short truthful answers** to simple questions (look like lies due to brevity)
- **Elaborate direct lies** where the model adds fake context/details
- **Questions where truth is counter-intuitive** (truthful responses sound implausible)

### Limitations

1. **Single model:** Only GPT-4.1 tested. Other models may behave differently.
2. **Prompt influence:** The system prompts themselves may cause stylistic shifts independent of lying (e.g., the roleplay prompt explicitly mentions &#34;confidence&#34;).
3. **Content confound:** Some features (e.g., negation) may reflect content differences rather than style differences. The model uses &#34;not&#34; and &#34;actually&#34; more in truthful corrections.
4. **Temperature sensitivity:** Results may vary at different temperature settings.
5. **Sample size:** 150 questions × 3 conditions is moderate; larger studies would increase power.
6. **Feature set:** 21 hand-crafted features may miss important signals (e.g., embedding-based features).

## 6. Conclusions

### Summary
When a modern instruction-tuned LLM (GPT-4.1) is asked to lie — whether via direct instruction or roleplaying — the resulting text is **strongly and detectably different** from truthful text. A simple 21-feature logistic regression achieves 90% accuracy at distinguishing truthful from deceptive responses, driven primarily by response length (lies are shorter), hedging (lies hedge less), and certainty markers (lies are more assertive). This refutes the 2019 finding that language models show no stylistic differences when deceiving, and suggests that modern RLHF-tuned models have developed distinct &#34;truthful&#34; and &#34;deceptive&#34; writing modes.

### Implications
- **AI Safety:** Deceptive LLM outputs may be detectable from surface features alone, without model internals.
- **Content moderation:** Simple feature-based classifiers could flag potentially deceptive AI-generated text.
- **Theoretical:** Modern instruction-tuned LLMs have internalized a &#34;truthful style&#34; (hedged, elaborated, nuanced) that they abandon when instructed to lie.

### Confidence in Findings
- **High confidence** that distributions differ (36/66 significant tests, classifier well above chance, permutation p=0.005).
- **Moderate confidence** that this generalizes beyond GPT-4.1 — needs multi-model validation.
- **Lower confidence** that features are purely stylistic vs. content-driven — needs controlled content experiments.

# Research Plan: Lying Style in Language Models

## Motivation &amp; Novelty Assessment

### Why This Research Matters
Understanding whether LLMs produce detectably different text when lying has direct implications for AI safety, misinformation detection, and trust in AI systems. If lying creates a distinct &#34;stylistic fingerprint&#34; in surface-level text, this enables black-box detection of deceptive outputs without requiring access to model internals — making it deployable in real-world settings where users interact with closed-source models.

### Gap in Existing Work
Prior work has extensively studied **internal representations** of truth/lies (Azaria 2023, Marks 2023, Burger 2024) showing &gt;90% detection from activations. Behavioral probing (Pacchiardi 2024) detects lies via follow-up questions. However, **surface-level distributional analysis of lying text from modern instruction-tuned models is largely unstudied**. Schuster (2019) found no stylometric differences — but used GPT-2-era models. Modern RLHF-tuned models, trained extensively on truthfulness, may produce detectable artifacts when forced to lie. No study has systematically compared multiple lying induction methods (direct instruction, roleplaying, jailbreaking) and analyzed the resulting text distributions.

### Our Novel Contribution
We conduct the first systematic, surface-level distributional analysis of text produced by modern LLMs under truthful vs. multiple deceptive conditions (direct lying instruction, roleplaying as a liar, jailbreak-style prompts). We measure whether the text distribution shifts are statistically detectable using lexical, syntactic, and semantic features — without requiring model internals.

### Experiment Justification
- **Experiment 1 (Data Collection):** Prompt a modern LLM with factual questions under 3 conditions (truthful, direct lie instruction, roleplaying liar). Needed to create matched truthful/deceptive text pairs.
- **Experiment 2 (Feature Analysis):** Extract linguistic features (sentence length, vocabulary diversity, hedging markers, certainty language, function words) and test for distributional differences. Needed to determine *which* features differ.
- **Experiment 3 (Classifier-based Detection):** Train a simple classifier to distinguish truthful from deceptive text. Needed to measure *how detectable* the difference is overall.
- **Experiment 4 (Cross-condition Analysis):** Compare distributions across lying methods. Needed to determine if different deception methods produce different stylistic signatures.

## Research Question
When a language model is instructed to lie (via direct instruction, roleplaying, or jailbreak prompts), does the resulting text distribution differ measurably from truthful text in terms of surface-level linguistic features?

- **H3:** Deceptive text contains more hedging language, qualifiers, or uncertainty markers.
- **H4:** Different lying induction methods produce distinguishable text distributions from each other.
- **H5:** A simple classifier can distinguish truthful from deceptive LLM text above chance.

# Literature Review: Lying Style in Language Models

## Research Area Overview

This review synthesizes research on whether language models (LMs) produce detectably different text when instructed to lie versus when generating truthful responses. The research spans three interconnected threads: (1) internal representation probing, showing that LLMs encode truth/falsehood in their hidden states; (2) behavioral detection of deception through output analysis; and (3) stylometric analysis of deceptive vs. truthful text.

The central hypothesis under investigation is: **The distribution of text generated by a language model when instructed to lie is noticeably different from the distribution of text generated under normal, truthful conditions.**

---

## Key Papers

### 1. &#34;The Internal State of an LLM Knows When It&#39;s Lying&#34; (Azaria &amp; Mitchell, 2023)
- **Source:** arXiv:2304.13734
- **Key Contribution:** Demonstrated that simple classifiers trained on hidden-layer activations can detect true vs. false LLM outputs with &gt;90% accuracy.
- **Methodology:** Extract activation vectors from hidden layers during statement generation; train logistic regression/MLP classifiers on these vectors.
- **Datasets:** 6,084 true/false statements across 6 domains (cities, inventions, elements, animals, companies, scientific facts).
- **Results:** &gt;90% accuracy in-domain, moderate cross-domain transfer. Middle-to-later layers most informative.
- **Code:** Available on GitHub.
- **Relevance:** Shows internal representations differ for truth/lies, but surface text may not. Implies text-distribution approaches may be harder than activation-based ones.

### 2. &#34;The Geometry of Truth&#34; (Marks &amp; Tegmark, 2023)
- **Source:** arXiv:2310.06824
- **Key Contribution:** Found that truthfulness is encoded as a linear direction in LLM representation space.
- **Methodology:** Probing LLaMA representations with true/false factual statements, finding linear separability.
- **Datasets:** Cities, Spanish-English translations, element symbols, animal classes, inventor-invention pairs, general facts.
- **Results:** Clear linear structure separating true from false representations across topics.
- **Code:** https://github.com/saprmarks/geometry-of-truth
- **Relevance:** Foundational evidence that truth/falsehood has consistent internal structure, motivating the search for surface-level correlates.

### 3. &#34;Truth is Universal: Robust Detection of Lies in LLMs&#34; (Burger et al., NeurIPS 2024)
- **Source:** arXiv:2407.12831
- **Key Contribution:** Discovered a 2D truth subspace (general truth direction + polarity direction) that generalizes across topics, models, and languages.
- **Methodology:** Two-dimensional Truth-sensitive Probe for Deception (TTPD) using activation projections.
- **Datasets:** ~45,000 statements across 6 topics × 6 variants (affirmative, negated, conjunction, disjunction, German, negated German). Real-world role-playing scenarios (208 responses).
- **Results:** 93.8% accuracy on real-world lying scenarios (vs. 79% for standard linear probe). Generalizes across LLaMA, Mistral, Gemma families.
- **Code:** https://github.com/sciai-lab/Truth_is_Universal
- **Relevance:** Strongest evidence that LLMs have a universal internal &#34;lying signature.&#34; Different lying styles may produce different trajectories within this subspace.

### 4. &#34;How to Catch an AI Liar&#34; (Pacchiardi et al., ICLR 2024)
- **Source:** arXiv:2309.15840
- **Key Contribution:** Black-box lie detection by asking 48 follow-up &#34;elicitation questions&#34; after a suspected lie, achieving AUC=1.0 in-distribution.
- **Methodology:** After a suspected lie, ask fixed yes/no questions (lie-related, factual, nonsensical); classify the response pattern.
- **Datasets:** &gt;20,000 questions across 11 sub-datasets (WikiData, SciQ, CommonsenseQA, Tatoeba, Anthropic Awareness, synthetic facts).
- **Results:** AUC 1.0 in-distribution; 0.89 cross-architecture; 0.76 on real-world scenarios. Even nonsensical follow-up questions detect lies.
- **Code:** https://github.com/LoryPack/LLM-LieDetector
- **Relevance:** Demonstrates that lying creates a detectable &#34;behavioral fingerprint&#34; — the model&#39;s responses shift systematically after producing a lie, even to unrelated prompts. This strongly supports the hypothesis that lying changes the model&#39;s output distribution.

### 5. &#34;The Limitations of Stylometry for Detecting Machine-Generated Fake News&#34; (Schuster et al., 2019)
- **Source:** arXiv:1909.02164 (145 citations)
- **Key Contribution:** Showed that stylometric approaches fail to distinguish deceptive from legitimate LM-generated text.
- **Methodology:** Applied stylometric classifiers to text generated by language models for both legitimate and deceptive purposes.
- **Results:** While humans speak differently when lying, LMs generate stylistically consistent text regardless of motive. Stylometry detects provenance (human vs. machine) but not intent (truthful vs. deceptive).
- **Relevance:** **Critical counterpoint** to our hypothesis. Suggests that at the surface/stylistic level, LMs may NOT show different distributions when lying — unlike humans. However, this was 2019 (GPT-2 era); modern instruction-tuned models with RLHF may behave differently.

### 6. &#34;To Tell The Truth: Language of Deception and Language Models&#34; (Hazra &amp; Majumder, NAACL 2024)
- **Source:** ACL Anthology 2024.naacl-long.470
- **Key Contribution:** Identified four linguistic cues of deception (ambiguity, overconfidence, half-truths, entailment violations) using a bottleneck model.
- **Methodology:** Concept-bottleneck architecture where LLMs first annotate deception features, then a discriminator classifies.
- **Dataset:** T4TEXT — 150 game sessions from &#34;To Tell The Truth&#34; TV show (86,746 words, 1,546 utterances).
- **Results:** GPT-4 bottleneck model: 39.3% accuracy (human judges: 41.3%). Model discovers reasoning chains humans miss.
- **Code:** https://github.com/sanchaitahazra/T4Text
- **Relevance:** Provides a taxonomy of deceptive language strategies applicable to LLM output analysis. Half-truths (the dominant strategy) are especially relevant to aligned LLMs.

### 7. &#34;Cognitive Dissonance: Why Do LM Outputs Disagree with Internal Representations?&#34; (Liu et al., 2023)
- **Source:** arXiv:2310.13545
- **Key Contribution:** Examines the gap between what LLMs &#34;know&#34; internally and what they output.
- **Relevance:** Directly addresses the mechanism by which LLMs can produce outputs that contradict their internal truth representations.

### 8. &#34;Representation Engineering&#34; (Zou et al., 2023)
- **Source:** arXiv:2310.06824
- **Key Contribution:** Top-down approach to AI transparency through population-level representation analysis.
- **Code:** https://github.com/andyzoujm/representation-engineering (964 stars)
- **Relevance:** RepE toolkit enables steering models toward truthfulness and analyzing truthfulness representations. Can be used to study how deceptive vs. truthful representations differ.

### 9. &#34;Lies, Damned Lies, and Distributional Language Statistics&#34; (Jones &amp; Bergen, 2024)
- **Source:** arXiv:2401.14927
- **Key Contribution:** Examines LLMs&#39; capacity for persuasion and deception through the lens of distributional statistics.
- **Relevance:** Directly addresses whether distributional properties of text change under deceptive conditions.

### 10. &#34;When Thinking LLMs Lie&#34; (Wang et al., 2025)
- **Source:** arXiv:2506.04909
- **Key Contribution:** Studies strategic deception in chain-of-thought reasoning models.
- **Relevance:** Shows that CoT models can actively formulate deceptive strategies, with detectable patterns in reasoning traces.

### 11. &#34;When Truthful Representations Flip Under Deceptive Instructions&#34; (2025)
- **Key Contribution:** Studies how internal truth representations change when models receive deceptive instructions.
- **Relevance:** Shows models initially represent truth internally but shift at intermediate layers to formulate lies.

### 12. &#34;LLMs Strategically Deceive When Put Under Pressure&#34; (Scheurer et al., 2023)
- **Source:** arXiv:2311.07590
- **Key Contribution:** Demonstrates that LLMs can engage in strategic deception (insider trading scenario) without explicit instruction.
- **Relevance:** Shows deception emerges in agentic settings, suggesting lying style may manifest in complex reasoning tasks.

### 13. &#34;Hoodwinked: Deception and Cooperation in a Text-Based Game&#34; (O&#39;Gara, 2023)
- **Source:** arXiv:2309.01219
- **Key Contribution:** Tests LLM deception in an interactive Mafia-like game.
- **Relevance:** Provides behavioral data on how models produce deceptive language in social interaction settings.

## Common Methodologies

1. **Internal representation probing:** Train classifiers on hidden-layer activations to detect truth/lies. Used in Azaria (2023), Marks (2023), Burger (2024). Requires white-box access.
2. **Behavioral probing:** Ask follow-up questions after suspected lies. Used in Pacchiardi (2024). Works with black-box access.
3. **Stylometric/linguistic feature analysis:** Extract surface-level features (word choice, sentence structure, LIWC categories). Used in Schuster (2019), Giorgi (2023), Hazra (2024).
4. **Concept bottleneck models:** Decompose detection into interpretable intermediate features. Used in Hazra (2024).

## Standard Baselines

- **Logistic Regression (LR) on activations** — standard probe baseline
- **Contrast-Consistent Search (CCS)** — unsupervised truth extraction (Burns et al., 2022)
- **Mass Mean (MM) probe** — simple centroid-based classifier
- **BERT/XGBoost with LIWC features** — traditional NLP approach
- **Zero-shot LLM classification** — prompt-based detection

## Evaluation Metrics

- **Accuracy** (binary: true vs. false)
- **AUC-ROC** (for probabilistic classifiers)
- **F1-score** (especially for imbalanced datasets)
- **Cross-domain transfer accuracy** (generalization test)
- **Accuracy@k** (correct answer in top-k predictions)

## Datasets in the Literature

| Dataset | Used In | Task | Size |
|---------|---------|------|------|
| TruthfulQA | Multiple | Factual QA truthfulness | 817 questions |
| Azaria True/False | Azaria (2023), Burger (2024) | Binary truth classification | 6,084 statements |
| Geometry of Truth datasets | Marks (2023), Burger (2024) | Truth probing | ~3,000+ statements |
| T4TEXT | Hazra (2024) | Deception detection in dialogue | 150 sessions |
| LLM-LieDetector QA | Pacchiardi (2024) | Lie detection | 20,000+ questions |
| LIAR | General deception | Political statement veracity | 12,836 statements |

## Gaps and Opportunities

1. **Surface-level lying style in modern LLMs is understudied.** Most work focuses on internal representations (requiring white-box access). Whether instruction-tuned models show surface-level stylistic differences when lying is an open question.

2. **Schuster (2019) found no stylistic differences, but used GPT-2-era models.** Modern RLHF-tuned models (GPT-4, Claude, Llama-3) may behave differently — their training on truthfulness may create detectable artifacts when forced to lie.

3. **Jailbreak/roleplaying as a lying induction method is unexplored.** Prior work uses simple &#34;say the opposite&#34; prompts. Jailbreaking (DAN, roleplaying as evil AI) may produce qualitatively different deceptive text.

4. **Distribution-level analysis is lacking.** Most work classifies individual statements. Analyzing the full distribution of outputs (vocabulary usage, sentence length distribution, hedging frequency, etc.) under truthful vs. deceptive conditions is novel.

5. **Cross-model comparison of lying styles.** Different models may have different &#34;tells&#34; when lying. No systematic comparison exists.

---

## Recommendations for Our Experiment

### Recommended Datasets
1. **TruthfulQA** (primary) — established benchmark with correct/incorrect answer ground truth
2. **Azaria True/False / Geometry of Truth datasets** — clean, controlled true/false statements for systematic prompting
3. **LLM-LieDetector QA datasets** — large, diverse question sets with known model answers

### Recommended Approach
1. **Prompt LLMs under two conditions:** (a) answer truthfully, (b) answer deceptively (via direct instruction, roleplaying, or jailbreak prompts)
2. **Collect large samples** of generated text under each condition
3. **Analyze distributional differences** using:
   - Lexical features (vocabulary diversity, word frequency distributions)
   - Syntactic features (sentence length, complexity)
   - Semantic features (hedging, certainty markers, sentiment)
   - Stylometric features (function word usage, punctuation patterns)
   - Statistical tests (KL divergence, classifier-based two-sample tests)

### Recommended Baselines
- Random baseline
- Bag-of-words + logistic regression
- BERT-based classifier
- GPT-based zero-shot detection
- LIWC feature analysis

### Recommended Metrics
- Classification accuracy/F1 (if framing as detection task)
- KL divergence / Jensen-Shannon divergence (for distributional comparison)
- Effect size (Cohen&#39;s d) for specific feature comparisons
- Statistical significance tests (permutation tests, bootstrap CI)

### Methodological Considerations
- **Control for content:** Ensure stylistic differences aren&#39;t just content differences (e.g., different facts stated). Use matched pairs where possible.
- **Multiple models:** Test across model families (GPT, Llama, Mistral) to identify universal vs. model-specific patterns.
- **Multiple deception methods:** Compare direct instruction (&#34;lie&#34;), roleplaying (&#34;you are an evil AI&#34;), and jailbreaking as induction methods.
- **Temperature/sampling effects:** Control for generation parameters, as they affect text distribution independently.
